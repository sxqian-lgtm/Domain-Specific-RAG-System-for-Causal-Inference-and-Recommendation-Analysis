A simple before after comparison within the treated users is not enough to evaluate the new
recommendation system. In the synthetic data, treated users drop from about 45.401 minutes
before treatment to about 44.361 after treatment, which by itself looks negative. However,
time effects unrelated to treatment may also change viewing behavior. The control group is
therefore needed to estimate the background trend. In this dataset, the control group moves
slightly upward over the same period, from about 44.487 to 44.687. DID uses that control
trend to adjust the treated group's before after change. This is the main reason naive
before after analysis can mislead recommendation teams: product launches, seasonality, and
other contextual shocks may happen at the same time as the treatment. Without a comparison
group, those forces are easy to confuse with causal impact.
