The central identifying assumption behind difference in differences is parallel trends.
It does not require the treated and control groups to have the same level before treatment.
Instead, it requires that in the absence of treatment, their average outcomes would have
moved in parallel over time. This matters because DID estimates the treatment effect by
subtracting the control group's time trend from the treated group's time trend. If the
treated group was already on a different trajectory before the intervention, then the DID
estimate mixes treatment impact with pre existing trend differences. In practice, analysts
often inspect pre treatment outcomes, compare business context across groups, and discuss
possible shocks that might break the assumption. A convincing DID analysis explains why
the control group is a good counterfactual trend for the treated group, not just why the
groups look similar at one point in time.
