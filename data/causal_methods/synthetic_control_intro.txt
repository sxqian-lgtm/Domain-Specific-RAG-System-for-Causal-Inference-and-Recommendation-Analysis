Synthetic control is a longitudinal causal inference method that builds a weighted combination
of control units to approximate the treated unit's pre-treatment behavior. It is especially
useful when the intervention affects an aggregate unit such as a city, region, state, or other
large entity, and when a simple treated-versus-control comparison is not credible. The method
can be viewed as an extension of the DID idea because it still tries to construct a
counterfactual trend for the treated case. The difference is that instead of using one raw
control group average, it uses a data-driven weighted combination of controls that better
matches the treated unit before treatment. In teaching terms, synthetic control is often
presented as one of the most important modern innovations in policy evaluation with
longitudinal data.
