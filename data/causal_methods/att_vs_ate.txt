ATT and ATE answer related but different causal questions. The average treatment effect,
ATE, is the average effect if everyone in the target population received treatment instead
of control. The average treatment effect on the treated, ATT, focuses only on units that
actually received treatment. ATT is often more realistic in product or recommendation
settings because treatment assignment is rarely random across the whole population. For
example, if a recommendation system is rolled out first to a subset of users, ATT asks how
much the system changed outcomes for those exposed users. ATE asks what would happen if the
same treatment logic were extended to all users. The distinction matters when treated users
differ systematically from untreated users. In those cases, ATT may be easier to estimate
credibly, while ATE may require stronger assumptions about transportability and overlap.
