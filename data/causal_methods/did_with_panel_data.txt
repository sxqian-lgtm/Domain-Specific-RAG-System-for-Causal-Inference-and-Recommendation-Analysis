Difference-in-differences is especially useful in longitudinal or panel-data settings where
the same units are observed across time. In this setting, each unit is measured before and
after treatment, and the analyst compares how treated units change relative to control units.
This is different from a cross-sectional comparison because time information is part of the
design. A key advantage of DID is that it can remove some unobserved confounding if those
unobserved differences are stable across time. That is why DID is widely used in economics,
policy analysis, and applied business studies. However, this does not mean DID solves every
confounding problem. It still relies on assumptions such as parallel trends and can fail when
group-specific shocks, changing composition, or non-comparable time dynamics are present.
