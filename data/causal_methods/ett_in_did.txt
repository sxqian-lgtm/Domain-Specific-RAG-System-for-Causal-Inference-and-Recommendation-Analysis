In the standard two-period DID setup, the causal target is often the effect of treatment on
the treated in the post-treatment period. This quantity is sometimes written as the effect on
the treated at time two, meaning the average difference between the treated units' observed
post-treatment outcomes and the outcomes those same units would have shown in the post period
without treatment. This framing matters because DID is usually not estimating a universal
population effect by default. It is focused on the treated units and their missing
counterfactual trend. That makes DID conceptually closer to ATT-style reasoning than to a fully
generalized ATE-style estimand. In project terms, this means DID is answering how the treated
group changed relative to its untreated counterfactual, not necessarily what would happen if
every possible user were forced into treatment.
