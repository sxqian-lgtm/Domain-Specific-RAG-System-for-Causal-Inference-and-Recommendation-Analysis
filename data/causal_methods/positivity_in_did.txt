Positivity in DID means that the comparison needed for estimation must be feasible in the data.
At a practical level, both treated and control groups need to exist in the relevant covariate
and time structure used for comparison. If certain types of units appear only in treatment or
only in control, then the counterfactual trend becomes hard to estimate credibly. Positivity is
often less emphasized in informal DID discussions than parallel trends, but it still matters.
Without enough overlap between the compared groups, even a correctly written DID formula may be
driven by extrapolation rather than genuine comparison. For recommendation analysis, positivity
problems can appear if the new system is deployed only to a narrow user segment with no
comparable untreated users.
