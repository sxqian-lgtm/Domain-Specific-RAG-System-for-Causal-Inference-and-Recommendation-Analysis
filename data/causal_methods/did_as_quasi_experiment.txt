Difference-in-differences is often described as a quasi-experimental method because it tries
to mimic the logic of an experiment using observational data. Instead of random assignment, it
relies on a treatment group and a control group observed over time, with at least one
pre-treatment period and one post-treatment period. The treatment effect is estimated by
comparing how outcomes change over time in the treated group relative to how they change in the
control group. This makes DID stronger than a simple cross-sectional comparison and stronger
than a simple before-after comparison. However, it is still not a true experiment, which is why
its validity depends on assumptions such as parallel trends and a credible comparison group.
