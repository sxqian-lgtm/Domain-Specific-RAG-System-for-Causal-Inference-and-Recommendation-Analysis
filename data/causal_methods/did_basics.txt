Difference-in-differences (DID) estimates a treatment effect by comparing changes over time
between a treated group and a comparison group. The key identifying assumption is parallel
trends: without treatment, the average outcome trend for the treated group would have moved
in parallel with the control group. A naive DID estimate can be biased when the groups have
different underlying trends, when treatment timing is correlated with potential outcomes, or
when other concurrent shocks affect one group differently from the other.
