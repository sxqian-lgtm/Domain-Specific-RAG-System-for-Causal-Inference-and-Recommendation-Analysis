Chapter 4 introduces counterfactuals, which ask what would have happened under an alternative
action for the same unit or situation. A counterfactual query is more specific than an
average intervention question because it combines evidence about what actually occurred with a
hypothetical change. For example, instead of asking what would happen on average if all users
received a new recommender, a counterfactual asks whether a particular user who did not
receive it would have watched more if they had. Counterfactuals are powerful because they
support explanation, attribution, and individualized reasoning. They are also more demanding
because they require a causal model that links observed evidence to latent disturbances and
alternative worlds. In project discussions, counterfactual language is often present even when
teams do not use the formal term.
