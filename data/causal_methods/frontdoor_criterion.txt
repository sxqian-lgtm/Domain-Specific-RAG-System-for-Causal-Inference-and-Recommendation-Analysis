The front-door criterion is a causal identification strategy used when backdoor adjustment is
not available because the main confounder is unobserved. The idea is to identify a mediator
that lies on the causal path from treatment to outcome and use that mediator to recover the
causal effect. This is more demanding than backdoor adjustment because several conditions must
hold: the treatment must affect the mediator, the mediator must affect the outcome, there must
be no unblocked backdoor path from treatment to mediator, and backdoor paths from mediator to
outcome must be blockable by observed variables. In the primer, smoking, tar deposits, and lung
cancer are used as the classic example. For this project, front-door is useful as a conceptual
extension showing that causal identification is not limited to ordinary covariate adjustment.
