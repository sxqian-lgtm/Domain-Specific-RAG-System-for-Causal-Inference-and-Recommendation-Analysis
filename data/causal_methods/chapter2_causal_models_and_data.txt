Chapter 2 of the primer is about how causal models show up in observed data through
patterns of dependence and independence. The main idea is that a directed acyclic graph is
not only a picture of assumed causal relations. It also implies testable statistical
restrictions. Some pairs of variables should be associated, while others should become
independent after conditioning on the right set. This matters for causal analysis because it
lets the analyst check whether a proposed model is at least broadly compatible with the
observed data. The chapter does not claim that data alone can prove the true causal graph.
Instead, it shows how graphical assumptions and data patterns talk to each other. For a RAG
assistant, this chapter is useful when users ask how graphs, observables, and conditional
independence are connected.
