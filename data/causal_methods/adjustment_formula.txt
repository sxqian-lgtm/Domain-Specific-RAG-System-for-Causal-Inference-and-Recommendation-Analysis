The adjustment formula is the bridge between a causal estimand and observable data once a
valid covariate set has been identified. Its logic is simple: estimate the outcome under
treatment and control within levels of the adjustment variables, then average those
quantities over the covariate distribution. This produces an estimate of an interventional
effect using observational information, provided the graphical assumptions are valid. The
formula is important because it explains why covariate adjustment is more than a modeling
habit. It is justified by a causal structure. When the adjustment set is wrong, the formula
can still produce biased answers even if the regression or propensity model looks
statistically strong. This is why the graph comes first and the estimator comes second.
