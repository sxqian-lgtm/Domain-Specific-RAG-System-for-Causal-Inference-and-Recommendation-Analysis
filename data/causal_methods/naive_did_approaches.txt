Teaching material on DID often contrasts the correct design with two naive alternatives. The
first naive approach ignores the control group and compares only the treated group's outcome
before and after treatment. This can be misleading because time-varying background factors may
change the outcome even without treatment. The second naive approach ignores the pre-treatment
period and compares treated and untreated units only in the post period. This can also be
misleading because baseline differences between groups may reflect confounding rather than
treatment effects. DID improves on both naive approaches by combining time comparison and group
comparison. In effect, it adjusts the treated group's before-after change by the control
group's time trend. This is why DID is often introduced as a design that corrects weaknesses in
simple before-after and simple treated-versus-control comparisons.
