One of the first lessons in causal inference is that correlation alone cannot justify a
causal claim. A positive association between two variables may arise because one causes the
other, because the causal direction runs the other way, or because a third variable affects
both. The primer by Pearl and coauthors emphasizes this point with simple examples, such as
mistakenly concluding that marriage raises income just because the two are positively
correlated. In recommendation systems, the same warning applies when highly engaged users
receive a new recommender and also watch more content. Without a causal design, the analyst
cannot tell whether the recommender caused extra viewing or whether already active users
were more likely to be assigned to treatment. This is why causal analysis requires an
explicit design, assumptions, and adjustment strategy rather than relying on raw
correlations.
