Selection bias often arises when the sample itself depends on variables that are influenced by
multiple upstream causes. In graphical terms, this is closely related to conditioning on a
collider or on a descendant of a collider. The primer connects this idea to Berkson's paradox:
two variables that are independent in the population can become associated after restricting the
analysis to a selected subgroup. This matters because many applied analyses do not only work
with naturally observed populations. They work with filtered samples, active users, retained
subscribers, or observed cases. In those settings, apparent relationships may be created by the
selection process itself rather than by a genuine causal connection. This concept is highly
relevant in recommendation analytics, where sample restrictions can easily distort conclusions.
