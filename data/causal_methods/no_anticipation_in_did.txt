An important DID assumption is no anticipation. This means treatment assignment should not
change the pre-treatment outcome. In other words, units should not begin responding to the
future treatment before the treatment actually starts. If people adjust their behavior in
advance, then the pre-treatment period is already contaminated and no longer represents a clean
baseline. In a product setting, this could happen if users learn that a new recommendation
system is coming and begin changing how they use the platform before rollout. DID relies on the
pre period reflecting untreated behavior, so anticipation effects can bias the estimate even if
the formal before-after structure is still present.
