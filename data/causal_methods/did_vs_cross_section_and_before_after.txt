One of the clearest ways to understand DID is to compare it with two simpler approaches. A
before-after design looks only at how the treated group changes across time, but it ignores the
fact that time itself may change outcomes. A cross-sectional treated-versus-control comparison
looks only at differences between groups after treatment, but it ignores baseline differences
that may already have existed before the intervention. DID combines both dimensions. It asks how
the treated group's change compares with the control group's change. This is why DID is often
explained as a method that removes bias from both simple time comparison and simple group
comparison, assuming the untreated trends are comparable.
