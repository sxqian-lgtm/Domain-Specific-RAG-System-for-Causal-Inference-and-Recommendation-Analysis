Although DID is designed to reduce bias, it can still fail in several common ways. If the
treatment and control groups do not satisfy parallel trends, the estimate mixes treatment impact
with underlying trend differences. If treatment assignment is related to outcome dynamics, DID
can still suffer from selection bias. Other important threats include omitted variable bias,
reverse causality in poorly defined designs, mean reversion, and shocks that affect one group
differently from the other. This is why DID should be treated as a causal design with
assumptions, not as an automatic correction tool. In practice, researchers often inspect pre
trends, discuss institutional context, and add robustness checks to reduce the risk of these
biases.
