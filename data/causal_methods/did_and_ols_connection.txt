Difference-in-differences can be written as an ordinary least squares regression with group and
time indicators and an interaction term between treatment-group membership and the post period.
In that setup, the coefficient on the interaction term represents the DID estimate. This is one
reason DID became so popular in applied work: the estimator can be expressed in a regression
form that is easy to compute. But the regression form does not replace the causal assumptions.
The interpretation of the coefficient still depends on assumptions such as parallel trends, no
anticipation, positivity, and a meaningful treatment-control comparison. It is therefore useful
to remember that OLS is a computational representation of the DID design, not the reason the
design is causal.
